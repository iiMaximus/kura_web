Kura vs Yuka: two scanners, different ways to read labels.

Yuka is a well-known scanner for food and cosmetic products. Kura is built for people who want concise ingredient context, source caveats, and a scanner that now covers both food and beauty labels.

Which scanner fits your use case?

Topic Kura Yuka
Product categories Food scanning plus cosmetic and beauty scanning, including skincare, makeup, haircare, fragrance, and personal care labels. Yuka's public website describes scanning and analysis for food products and cosmetics.
Best fit People who want quick label translation, additive and ingredient context, and visible caveats about data limits. People who want an established scanner with product ratings and recommendations as described by Yuka.
Food approach Focuses on ingredient lists, additives, allergens, processing cues, and source-linked context. Yuka publicly describes evaluating food quality and showing ratings for scanned products.
Beauty approach Explains cosmetic ingredient roles and concern categories while emphasizing concentration, exposure, and personal sensitivity caveats. Yuka publicly describes cosmetic analysis that can identify categories such as endocrine disruptors, irritants, and allergens.
Decision caveat Kura pages emphasize that scans are educational and not medical, nutrition, allergy, or dermatology advice. Users should review Yuka's current app, methodology, and terms directly because product behavior can change.
How To Choose

Use the scanner that matches the question you are asking.

Use Kura when you want a concise explanation of ingredients and concern categories, especially if you want both food and beauty labels in one app. Use Yuka if its current rating style, product coverage, and recommendation flow better match how you shop.

For either app, the right habit is the same: scan, read the explanation, check the package, and seek professional guidance for allergies, medical conditions, pregnancy, children, or skin disease.

No app can replace the product label.

Food and cosmetic scanners are useful because labels are hard to read quickly. They are not a substitute for the current package, manufacturer instructions, recalls, clinician guidance, or personal allergy and skin history.
