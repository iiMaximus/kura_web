## Core Founder Narrative

Kura is based on a simple shopping behavior: people increasingly read labels, but labels are difficult to interpret in the moment. A shopper may compare a snack, a sunscreen, a serum, and a deodorant in the same trip, but the tools for understanding food ingredients and beauty ingredients have often been separate.

Kura brings those label-reading moments into one scanner. It helps users scan food, cosmetic, and beauty products, then turns ingredient lists into practical context while keeping limits clear: the app is educational and does not replace professional medical, nutrition, allergy, or dermatology advice.

## Pitch Angles

### 1. The Label-Reading Habit Has Moved Beyond Food

Consumers are applying the same questions to snacks, skincare, makeup, sunscreen, and personal care: What is in this, and what does the label mean? Kura's food plus cosmetics scanning makes that behavior easier to cover as one consumer trend.

Best for: consumer trends, retail, wellness, beauty, grocery, app discovery.

### 2. One Scanner For Grocery And Beauty Aisles

Most shoppers do not think in app categories while shopping. They want quick clarity when they pick up a packaged food or a skincare product. Kura positions ingredient scanning as a cross-category shopping behavior.

Best for: app reviews, product roundups, iOS newsletters, startup launch outlets.

### 3. AI Should Translate Labels, Not Pretend To Be A Doctor

Kura can be discussed as part of the broader move toward AI-assisted consumer tools, with a careful boundary: AI explanations can help make labels easier to understand, but they should not replace source data, product labels, or professional advice.

Best for: AI newsletters, consumer tech reporters, health-tech commentary.

### 4. Ingredient Transparency Without Fear Marketing

Kura can explain ingredient roles and concern categories without declaring every flagged product dangerous. This is especially useful for cosmetics, where exposure, concentration, frequency, and personal sensitivity matter.

Best for: cosmetic chemists, dermatology-adjacent creators, beauty editors.

### 5. Practical Help For Families Who Already Read Labels

Families with allergies, sensitivities, or dietary preferences often already read labels carefully. Kura can be framed as a helper that makes label review faster, while avoiding any claim that it guarantees safety or replaces allergy guidance.

Best for: parenting media, allergy-aware communities, school wellness resources.

### 6. Shopping Decisions Are Happening At Shelf Speed

The story is not only about health. It is about the pressure of making small product decisions quickly in stores, pharmacies, and online carts. Kura gives shoppers a faster way to compare labels and ask better questions.

Best for: retail, consumer behavior, product design, shopping technology.

### 7. Food And Beauty Apps Are Converging Around Product Data

Ingredient databases, barcode scans, public regulatory references, and AI explanations are becoming part of mainstream shopping tools. Kura gives reporters a consumer-facing example that spans both food and beauty.

Best for: data, open product databases, civic tech, consumer protection.
